I was re-watching *John Wick* the other day (for the umpteenth time, don’t judge), and it finally clicked why this 2014 sleeper hit still feels like a breath of fresh air. It wasn’t just the sleek suits or the "don't touch the dog" motivation—it was something much rarer in modern cinema: **The camera actually stayed still.**

Lately, action movies have developed a twitch. It’s become a trend to use **quick cuts** to manufacture a sense of "pace," but let’s call it what it really is: **nauseating.** When you see ten cuts in a six-second window, it’s a red flag. It usually means the director was too tired to actually choreograph a scene progression. Instead of storyboarding a masterpiece, they just shoved three cameras in a room and prayed the editor could find a way to make it look like "action" later. It’s the cinematic equivalent of a **Buzzfeed "article"** that consists of ten random GIFs and three lines of text. If you can’t tell a story without a jump cut every half-second, are you even making a movie?

If there’s a patron saint of this frantic mess, it’s **Michael Bay.** Whether it’s *Transformers* or *TMNT*, the issue is the same: the cuts jump between characters so fast you lose all emotional stakes. When you can’t tell who is punching whom—or why—you stop rooting for the hero and start rooting for the Advil.

*John Wick* works because it respects the audience's eyes. You see the movement, you feel the impact, and you follow the flow. It’s elegant, it’s deliberate, and most importantly, it doesn't require a barf bag. To the directors out there: please, for the love of cinema, **put down the scissors and let the camera roll.**
